Why it matters

It doesn't stay where
you left it.

Litter travels. Rain carries it into stormwater drains. Wind takes it to parks and reserves. Waterways deliver it to the sea. What begins as one piece of rubbish on a footpath can end up on a beach 300 kilometres away — or inside a seabird's stomach. Nothing disappears. It just moves.

Small habits.
Real change.

Awareness isn't enough. The rubbish problem is solved by consistent, ordinary actions taken by millions of people — not by grand gestures. Here's what actually makes a difference.

  • Always use a bin. If one isn't nearby, keep your rubbish with you until you find one.
  • Separate your waste correctly at home. Contaminated recycling goes to landfill regardless.
  • Pick up one piece of litter you didn't drop. Every time. Make it a habit, not an occasion.
  • Talk about it. Social norms change when people start finding littering unacceptable — not just unsightly.
  • Support businesses and councils that reduce packaging and invest in infrastructure.

Practical steps

What you can do
starting today.

None of this requires money, a campaign, or a lifestyle change. It requires a decision.

01
🗑️
Bin it. Every time.

No exceptions. No "just this once." The bin is almost always nearby. This is the single most impactful individual habit change you can make — because it's guaranteed to help.

02
♻️
Recycle properly.

Wishful recycling — putting something in the recycling bin hoping it's recyclable — contaminates entire loads. Check what's accepted in your council area. When in doubt, general waste.

03
👋
Say something.

Politely. Not confrontationally. Littering becomes less socially acceptable when people are willing to gently challenge it. You don't need to lecture — just notice it, aloud, to the people around you.

04
📢
Spread awareness.

Share the facts. Post about it. Talk about it at dinner. The rubbish problem persists partly because most people assume someone else is dealing with it. You can change that assumption.

05
🏘️
Join a clean-up.

Clean Up Australia Day, local council programs, neighbourhood groups. One morning per year with your community removes real rubbish from real places — and builds the habit of caring.

06
✉️
Contact your council.

Report overflowing bins. Advocate for better infrastructure in poorly-served areas. Councils respond to resident pressure — and inadequate bin access is a genuine part of the problem.

Spread the word

Tell someone who
needs to hear it.

The most effective way to change behaviour is peer-to-peer. Share this page with someone in your life — a friend, a colleague, your kids' school community.

Project Reflection

Our Journey

"Our solution evolved from a simple idea into a larger awareness campaign."

At the beginning of this project, our original idea was to create posters that would inform people about the rubbish problem. While posters can be effective for sharing information, we realised they would only reach a limited audience and would not provide much interaction.

As we developed our project, we wanted to take more action rather than simply displaying information. We decided to create an interactive website that could educate people through facts, statistics, quizzes and a pledge system.

Instead of abandoning our poster idea, we combined both solutions. The posters help attract attention and direct people to our website, while the website provides much more detailed information and opportunities for people to get involved.

One challenge we encountered was finding a way to make people engage with the issue instead of simply reading about it. By combining posters with an interactive website and pledge system, we created a solution that encourages people to learn, reflect and take action.
